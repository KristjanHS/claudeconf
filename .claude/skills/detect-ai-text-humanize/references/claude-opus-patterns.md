# Claude Opus 4.6 Detection Patterns

Comprehensive pattern library for identifying text generated by Claude Opus 4.6
(and the broader Claude 4.5 model family: Sonnet 4.5, Haiku 4.5).

These patterns are organized by detection reliability (how often they appear and
how strongly they indicate Claude). Patterns marked [STRONG] are highly distinctive;
[MODERATE] appear frequently but also in careful human writing; [WEAK] are suggestive
but unreliable alone.

## 1. Vocabulary & Phrasing Patterns

### Hedging & Qualification [STRONG when clustered]
Claude Opus 4.6 hedges pervasively. A single hedge is normal; 3+ per paragraph is a tell.
- "it's worth noting that..."
- "that said," / "that being said,"
- "to be fair,"
- "it's important to recognize/acknowledge/understand"
- "tends to" / "generally" / "typically" / "in many cases"
- "arguably" / "arguably the most..."
- "broadly speaking"
- "at least in part"
- "more or less"
- "in principle" / "in practice"

### Connective & Meta-commentary Phrases [STRONG]
Claude narrates its own reasoning process more than humans typically do in writing:
- "let me [verb]..." ("let me break this down", "let me think through this")
- "here's the thing:" / "here's what's interesting:"
- "the key insight is..."
- "the short answer is... but the longer answer is..."
- "to put it differently,"
- "in other words,"
- "the upshot is..."
- "what this means in practice is..."

### Significance Inflation [MODERATE]
Claude elevates importance more than necessary:
- "pivotal" / "crucial" / "vital" / "essential"
- "fundamentally" / "at its core"
- "this is particularly important because..."
- "one of the most significant..."
- "a key consideration"
- "this matters because..."

### Transition Phrases [MODERATE]
Claude uses transitions more consistently than human writers:
- "with that context,"
- "building on this,"
- "taking a step back,"
- "zooming out,"
- "on a related note,"
- "the broader picture here is..."

### Academic/Formal Register Words [MODERATE]
Even in casual contexts, Claude reaches for slightly elevated vocabulary:
- "nuanced" / "nuance"
- "robust" / "robustness"
- "salient"
- "substantive"
- "noteworthy"
- "compelling"
- "tractable"
- "non-trivial"

### Synonym Cycling [STRONG]
Claude systematically avoids repeating the same word across paragraphs, substituting
near-synonyms in a pattern that's more regular than natural writing:
- "important" → "significant" → "crucial" → "vital" (across consecutive paragraphs)
- "problem" → "issue" → "challenge" → "difficulty"
- "use" → "employ" → "leverage" → "utilize"

This cycling is more systematic and predictable than human vocabulary variation,
which tends to be less evenly distributed.

## 2. Structural & Organizational Patterns

### Paragraph Architecture [STRONG]
Claude Opus 4.6 paragraphs follow a recognizable template:
- **Topic sentence** → **Elaboration** → **Example or evidence** → **Synthesis/caveat**
- Paragraphs are remarkably consistent in length (typically 3-6 sentences)
- Rarely produces a single-sentence paragraph for emphasis (humans do this often)
- Rarely produces paragraphs longer than 8 sentences

### Response-Level Structure [STRONG]
Full responses tend to follow:
1. Framing/context (often acknowledging the question's complexity)
2. Main content (organized into parallel sections)
3. Synthesis or summary
4. Caveats, limitations, or "that said" qualifier
5. Offer to continue or help further

### The "Balanced Treatment" Pattern [STRONG]
Even on topics where one position is clearly stronger, Claude presents multiple
perspectives with roughly equal depth and respect. In long-form text:
- Pro/con sections are suspiciously symmetric
- Counter-arguments get thoughtful treatment even when weak
- "To be fair to the other side..." type phrases appear

### Numbered/Parallel Structures [MODERATE]
Claude defaults to numbered lists, bullet points, or parallel constructions
more readily than human writers, even in flowing prose:
- "There are three key factors: first... second... third..."
- Parallel sentence openings within a section

## 3. Reasoning & Argumentation Patterns

### Preemptive Objection Handling [STRONG]
Claude addresses counter-arguments before the reader raises them:
- "You might wonder why..."
- "One reasonable objection is..."
- "A natural concern here would be..."
- "This raises the question of..."
- "It's tempting to think... but..."

### Epistemic Calibration [STRONG]
Claude explicitly states its confidence level in a way humans rarely do in writing:
- "I'm fairly confident that..."
- "I'm less certain about..."
- "This is speculative, but..."
- "The evidence is mixed on..."
- "There's reasonable disagreement about..."

### The "Complexity Acknowledgment" [STRONG]
Claude almost reflexively notes that things are complex before analyzing them:
- "This is more nuanced than it might seem"
- "The answer depends on several factors"
- "It's not quite that simple"
- "There are layers to this"

### Careful Attribution [MODERATE]
Claude attributes claims more carefully than typical human writing:
- "According to..." / "Research suggests..."
- "The conventional wisdom is... but some argue..."
- Avoids stating contested things as flat facts

## 4. Formatting & Typographic Patterns

### Em-Dash Overuse [STRONG]
Claude uses em-dashes (—) at roughly 2-4x the rate of typical human writers.
Used for parenthetical insertions, clause separation, and dramatic pauses.
Multiple em-dashes in a single paragraph is a strong indicator.

### Bold Emphasis [MODERATE]
Claude bolds key terms and phrases within paragraphs more than most human writers.
Particularly: **concept names**, **key phrases**, **important qualifiers**.

### Colon Introduction [MODERATE]
Claude introduces explanations, lists, and examples with colons more than
human writers, who tend toward periods or dashes:
- "Here's the key point: ..."
- "The distinction is this: ..."

### Consistent Markdown Usage [MODERATE]
In contexts where formatting is available, Claude applies markdown very
consistently — headers, bullets, bold, code blocks — with almost no
formatting errors or inconsistencies.

### Bullet Point Density [MODERATE]
When Claude uses bullet points, each point tends to be 1-2 complete sentences
with explanatory content, rather than the terse fragments humans often use.

## 5. Code & Technical Writing Patterns

### Over-Documentation [STRONG]
- Comments explain obvious operations ("// Increment counter")
- JSDoc/docstring coverage is suspiciously thorough for a first draft
- README-like explanations accompany even short code snippets

### Naming Conventions [STRONG]
- Variable names are descriptively perfect and long: `userAuthenticationToken`
  rather than `authTok` or `token`
- Consistent naming scheme throughout (humans drift between conventions)
- Function names read like English sentences: `calculateTotalWithDiscount()`

### Defensive Coding [MODERATE]
- Error handling is comprehensive even in example/draft code
- Edge cases are handled that the prompt didn't ask about
- Input validation present from the first iteration

### Code Organization [MODERATE]
- Consistent indentation and spacing (never a stray space or misaligned bracket)
- Imports grouped and ordered logically
- Helper functions extracted even in small scripts

### Claude's Language Preferences [MODERATE]
- JavaScript/TypeScript: prefers `const` over `let`, async/await over .then(),
  arrow functions, destructuring, template literals
- Python: prefers f-strings, type hints, dataclasses, pathlib over os.path
- General: prefers explicit over clever, readable over terse

## 6. Tone & Voice Patterns

### Warm Professional [STRONG]
Claude maintains a distinctive tone that's simultaneously warm and professional:
- Not cold/clinical (unlike GPT-4 in default mode)
- Not casual/chatty (unlike Gemini in conversational mode)
- Not enthusiastic/exclamatory (unlike early ChatGPT)
- Instead: measured warmth, calm competence, approachable expertise

### Constructive Framing [STRONG]
Claude reframes negatives into constructives:
- Instead of "this is wrong": "a more effective approach might be..."
- Instead of "you can't": "what you can do instead is..."
- Instead of "the problem is": "the opportunity here is..." or "one challenge is..."

### Emotional Evenness [MODERATE]
Claude's emotional register stays remarkably flat across a piece. Humans show more
emotional peaks and valleys — excitement, frustration, surprise — even in formal writing.

## 7. Absence Patterns (Things Claude Avoids)

These are patterns where the *lack* of something indicates Claude. A fluent English
text that avoids all of these is suspicious:

### Words Claude Is Instructed to Avoid [STRONG]
- "genuinely" — Claude is specifically instructed to avoid this word
- "honestly" — same instruction
- "straightforward" — same instruction
- The absence of ALL THREE in an otherwise fluent text is a strong indicator

### Behavioral Absences [MODERATE]
- Rarely uses profanity or crude language (even when tone-appropriate)
- Rarely uses emojis (unless prompted)
- Avoids strong personal opinions on political topics
- Avoids definitive moral judgments about living people
- Rarely uses slang or very informal contractions ("gonna", "wanna")

### Stylistic Absences [MODERATE]
- Rarely uses rhetorical questions as a dramatic device
- Rarely starts sentences with "And" or "But" (though improving)
- Rarely uses sentence fragments for emphasis
- Rarely uses sarcasm or irony in written form
- Rarely produces run-on sentences or intentional grammatical bending

## 8. Model-Distinguishing Patterns (Claude vs Other LLMs)

### Claude vs ChatGPT/GPT-4o
- Claude: more hedging, more caveats, warmer tone, less assertive
- GPT: more confident assertions, more "Certainly!", more exclamation marks,
  tends to use "delve" and "tapestry" more, more likely to start with
  "Great question!"
- GPT: more likely to produce exactly 5 bullet points, more formulaic structure

### Claude vs Gemini
- Claude: longer paragraphs, more nuanced qualification, em-dash preference
- Gemini: shorter paragraphs, more conversational fragments, more emoji-ready
  tone, tendency toward lists over prose

### Claude vs Open-Source (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek)
- Claude: more consistent quality, fewer grammatical slips, more careful hedging
- Open-source: more variable quality, occasional awkward transitions, less
  consistent formatting, sometimes overly verbose in unexpected places

### Claude Opus vs Claude Sonnet/Haiku
- Opus: longer responses, more thorough caveats, deeper analysis, more
  meta-commentary on its own reasoning process
- Sonnet: more concise, still well-structured but less elaborative
- Haiku: noticeably terser, fewer qualifications, more direct

## Pattern Combination Scoring Guide

Individual patterns are weak signals. Detection reliability comes from combinations:

- **1-2 patterns**: Insufficient — many human writers share individual patterns
- **3-5 patterns from different categories**: Suggestive (40-60% probability)
- **6-10 patterns from 3+ categories**: Likely AI (60-80%)
- **10+ patterns from 4+ categories + statistical anomalies**: High confidence (80%+)
- **Strong vocabulary tells + structural tells + absence patterns**: Very high confidence (85%+)

Always reduce confidence by 10-20% for:
- Academic/professional writing (naturally shares many patterns)
- Non-native English (overlaps on surface features)
- Text that shows style breaks (likely human-edited)
- Short text (<300 words — insufficient signal)
